Results

Participants

Data were collected from 54 participants, of whom 48 (n = 29 Trauma - No Ongoing Impact; n = 19 Trauma - Ongoing Impact) completed both trauma assessment scales and the full RLWM task and were included in group-level analyses (Table 1). Six participants were excluded: four due to incomplete task sessions (<100 trials) and two due to duplicate session records. All remaining participants reported at least one significant life event on the LESS measure, indicating universal trauma exposure within the sample.

The two groups did not differ significantly in the total number of significant events recognised on the LESS; however, they differed substantially in persisting symptom severity across all IES-R subscales (Table 2), confirming the intended psychological distinction between groups. This pattern reflects the characteristics of the recruited sample rather than selective attrition and allows comparison of variability in current psychological impact among trauma-exposed individuals. Distributional characteristics were examined for all behavioural dependent variables on the task. Levene's tests confirmed homogeneity of variance for both accuracy, F(1, 46) = 0.13, p = .721, and reaction time, F(1, 46) = 0.07, p = .799. Shapiro–Wilk tests indicated violations of normality for accuracy in both trauma groups (both ps < .001) and for reaction time in the Trauma - Ongoing Impact group (p = .017). Inspection of distribution statistics showed moderate skewness typical of cognitive task data, with reaction time positively skewed and accuracy negatively skewed due to ceiling effects. Given the balanced group design (N = 48) and the robustness of ANOVA to normality violations under these conditions, parametric analyses were retained. Detailed distribution statistics are reported in Supplementary Table S2, and distributional plots are provided in Supplementary Figures S1 and S2.

Behavioural Performance

Overall Learning and Load Effects

Participants demonstrated reliable learning of stimulus-response associations across both load conditions, with mean accuracy significantly exceeding chance performance (33.3%) in all cases (Figure 1). A 2 × 2 mixed ANOVA revealed a strong main effect of load on accuracy, with performance higher in low-load blocks than in high-load blocks, F(1, 46) = 82.81, p < .001, ηp² = .64 (Figure 2A; Table 3). This effect was highly consistent at the individual level, with 45 of 48 participants (93.8%) showing greater accuracy under low load.

Reaction times (RT) showed a parallel pattern, with significantly faster responses in low-load compared to high-load conditions, F(1, 46) = 126.04, p < .001, ηp² = .73 (Figure 2B; Table 3). Together, these findings indicate that increasing working-memory load reduced learning efficiency and slowed response selection. Distributional properties of accuracy and reaction time underlying these analyses are reported in Supplementary Table S2.

Trauma Group Comparisons

There were no significant differences in behavioural performance between trauma groups. When performance was averaged across load conditions, there was no evidence of an overall effect of trauma group on accuracy, F(1, 46) = 0.06, p = .812, ηp² = .001, or reaction time, F(1, 46) = 2.55, p = .117, ηp² = .053 (Table 3). Participants with ongoing psychological impact performed comparably to those without ongoing symptoms across both load conditions.

The Load × Group interaction was also non-significant for accuracy, F(1, 46) < 0.001, p = .995, ηp² < .001, and RT, F(1, 46) < 0.001, p = .981, ηp² < .001 (Figure 2), indicating that the performance cost associated with increased cognitive load was equivalent across trauma groups.

Trauma Scores as Continuous Predictors

To examine whether individual differences in trauma exposure or symptom severity predicted task performance, regression analyses were conducted using continuous trauma indices. Neither total traumatic events (LESS Total) nor ongoing symptom severity (IES-R Total) significantly predicted overall accuracy (LESS: β = -0.003, p = .501; IES-R: β = -0.001, p = .283; Figure 3; Supplementary Table S1). Additional models including IES-R subscales (Intrusion, Avoidance, Hyperarousal) likewise yielded non-significant associations (all ps > .36). This pattern remained unchanged when low- and high-load conditions were analysed separately, indicating no detectable relationship between trauma severity and observable behavioural performance.
